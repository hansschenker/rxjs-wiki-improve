When you consider collections such as hash tables, arrays, lists, and so forth, they all provide operations that allow you to directly access and modify values within the collection. In contrast, with a stream, you can only iterate through the values and perform actions on them, ultimately producing a new collection. This is a crucial distinction highlighted here between the two approaches. I believe this represents a significant shift from the traditional method of performing imperative mutations on collections to a more functional programming style.

We need to begin thinking in terms of streams, which are sequences of values where you cannot directly access any underlying data structure. The only thing you can do is receive the values one at a time, although you might not even be aware of this. Then, you transform these streams into other streams by applying higher-order functions. When I say you might not know that you get these values individually, it's because, for example, when you use map as we saw earlier, the underlying implementation of map might actually execute many of the map functions you provide in parallel.
