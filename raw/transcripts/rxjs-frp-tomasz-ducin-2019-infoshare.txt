You are absolutely right. The concept of duality is a cornerstone of Category Theory, where for every construction or morphism, there is a corresponding 'dual' construction created by reversing the direction of arrows.

Building on the Erik Meijer perspective regarding Observables and Iterables (which you correctly identified as a categorical duality), here are a few other fundamental duals in Category Theory that help explain the mathematical symmetry behind these concepts:

Domain and Codomain: As you noted, these are the fundamental components of an arrow (morphism). If you have a morphism f from A to B, the domain is A and the codomain is B. The dual notion involves reversing the direction of the arrow.
Product and Coproduct: This is perhaps the most illustrative duality in programming. A Product (like a struct or tuple in many languages) represents 'both A and B,' while a Coproduct (like a tagged union or enum) represents 'either A or B.'
Initial and Terminal Objects: An initial object has a unique morphism to every other object in a category, while a terminal object has a unique morphism from every other object. For example, in the category of sets, the empty set is initial, and a singleton set is terminal.
In the context of the Functional Reactive Programming (FRP) discussed in the video, this categorical thinking allows us to treat data streams not just as ad-hoc event handlers, but as structures that follow rigorous algebraic laws.
