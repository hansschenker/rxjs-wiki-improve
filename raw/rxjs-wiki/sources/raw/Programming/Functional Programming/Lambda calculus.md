## Origins and Core Concepts of Lambda Calculus
- [[Lambda calculus]] is a formal system in mathematical logic, introduced by mathematician [[Alonzo Church]] in his 1932 paper "[[A Set of Postulates for the Foundation of Logic]]," which provides a notation for defining functions symbolically through the abstraction and application of functions using variable binding and substitution.
- The system's core mechanisms include the lambda operator λ, parentheses for grouping, and an infinite set of variables, with well-formed expressions built recursively to represent computable operations without reference to specific data types or machines, and it has evolved through Church's subsequent works, including a 1933 revision of his postulates and the 1941 monograph The Calculi of Lambda-Conversion.
- Lambda calculus is Turing-complete, meaning it can simulate any Turing machine and thus compute all effectively calculable functions, as proven by [[Alan Turing]] in 1937 through a direct correspondence between lambda-definable functions and those computable by his abstract machines, which underpins the Church-Turing thesis, positing that lambda calculus captures the intuitive notion of mechanical computation.
- The system has three primary constructs, including variables, abstractions denoted λx.M, which bind a variable x to a body term M to form a function, and applications, written as (M N), which combine two terms M and N to invoke M on N, and it has been extended to typed lambda calculus, introduced by Church in 1940 to avoid paradoxes such as Russell's by restricting variable types.

## Syntax and Fundamental Constructs
- [[Lambda calculus]] has profoundly influenced functional programming languages, where higher-order functions and currying derive directly from lambda abstractions, and it remains central to programming language semantics, proof assistants, and theoretical computer science, with its role in proving the undecidability of certain problems in number theory and its ability to model infinite computation.
- The lambda calculus system ensures confluence in the untyped variant, allowing unique normal forms for reducible terms, though some expressions like the self-applicator (λx.x x)(λx.x x) exhibit non-termination, highlighting the system's ability to model infinite computation, and it has been developed amid efforts to formalize the foundations of mathematics and logic in the 1930s.
- The lambda calculus is a foundational framework that underpins the design of functional programming languages, such as [[Haskell]] and [[Lisp (programming language) | Lisp]], by providing a theoretical basis for higher-order functions and type systems, and it also plays a key role in proof theory through connections like the Curry-Howard isomorphism.

## Applications in Functional Programming and Logic
- Lambda calculus has numerous applications in mathematics and computing, including the encoding of natural numbers, booleans, and recursive functions, allowing complex mathematical structures to be represented purely in terms of lambda terms, and it enables the formalization of mathematical proofs as computational objects.
- The Curry-Howard isomorphism, which equates proofs in intuitionistic logic with typed lambda terms, is central to the mathematical significance of [[Lambda calculus | lambda calculus]], and Alonzo Church's thesis, proposed in 1936, posits that the lambda-definable functions precisely capture the notion of effective computability, equivalent to the recursive functions identified by Kurt Gödel and others.
- Lambda calculus forms the basis for denotational semantics, where programs are interpreted as mathematical functions in domain-theoretic models, and it influences type theory by providing the computational framework for simply typed and higher-order typed systems, such as the simply typed lambda calculus, which ensures type safety and supports polymorphic typing in modern type systems.

## Syntax, Terms, and Variable Binding

- Lambda calculus addressed concerns over impredicative definitions, which are definitions that quantify over a totality including the defined entity itself, and it was able to do so by permitting such definitions through its expressive power with explicit lambda abstractions, although it does not fully avoid paradoxes without the use of typed variants.
- The untyped lambda calculus was later supplemented by Church's development of typed variants, which impose restrictions to ensure predicative definitions and consistency, thereby avoiding the paradoxes that can arise from impredicative definitions in systems like [[Bertrand Russell | Russell]] and Whitehead's Principia Mathematica.
- Lambda terms are the basic building blocks of lambda calculus, and they represent functions that accept an input and produce a result, with a lambda abstraction denoted as λx.M, where M is a lambda term that may depend on the input x, and this notation allows for anonymous function creation.
- The application of lambda terms occurs when one term is used as a function applied to another, and this process involves substituting the argument into the function's body, which builds intuition for computation as successive substitutions, and lambda terms treat functions as first-class entities that can be passed as arguments to other functions or returned as results.
- Lambda calculus has the power to model composition and iteration purely through functions, as highlighted by constructions such as higher-order functions, and it inherently supports currying, which is the technique of representing multi-argument functions through nested single-argument abstractions, allowing for flexible composition in functional expressions.

## Beta-Reduction and Evaluation Strategies
- In [[Lambda calculus | lambda calculus]], variables in a term are classified as either free or bound based on their relation to lambda abstractions, where a bound variable is one that occurs within the scope of a lambda binder and serves as a parameter to the function defined by that abstraction.
- The sets of free and bound variables in a term M, denoted FV(M) and BV(M) respectively, are defined inductively over the structure of lambda terms, with specific definitions provided for variables, applications, and abstractions.
- For example, in the term λx.xy, the variable x is bound by the outer λx, while the variable y is free, as it lies outside the scope of any λ-binder and must be provided from the term's context.
- The definitions of free and bound variables capture that free variables are those unbound by any enclosing abstraction, while bound variables are precisely those captured by lambdas in the term, and a key property of bound variables is that renaming them does not alter the term's meaning.
- Alpha-equivalence is an equivalence relation that identifies terms differing only in the names of their bound variables, allowing consistent renaming without altering the term's meaning, and two terms M and N are alpha-equivalent if N can be obtained from M by a sequence of renamings of bound variables.
- Alpha-equivalence is based on the idea that the specific names chosen for bound variables do not affect the logical structure of the term, and it forms the basis for substitution and other operations in lambda calculus, where care must be taken to avoid variable capture and ensure that the term's meaning remains intact.

## Eta-Conversion and Extensionality
- The [[Lambda calculus | lambda calculus]] relation, denoted as A[x := y], is reflexive, symmetric, and transitive, ensuring that alpha-equivalent terms represent the same function regardless of variable naming conventions, and this relation is crucial for substitution in lambda calculus.
- Substitution in lambda calculus, denoted as [N/x]M, replaces the free occurrences of variable x in term M with term N, and it must be capture-avoiding to prevent free variables in N from becoming inadvertently bound by lambdas in M, which is achieved through alpha-conversion if necessary.
- The formal definition of substitution proceeds recursively, where [N/x]y = N if y = x, and y otherwise, and for more complex terms, such as N/x and N/x, the substitution is defined in terms of the substitutions of the subterms.

## Normal Forms and Confluence Properties
- Capture-avoiding substitution ensures semantic correctness by renaming bound variables in M via alpha-conversion if necessary, avoiding conflicts where a free variable in N matches a binder in M, and this is particularly important in cases where the substitution could lead to capture, such as in the example of v/x.
- Substitution can be viewed as either parallel or sequential, with parallel substitution replacing multiple free variables simultaneously in a single pass, and sequential substitution applying replacements one at a time, requiring careful freshness checks at each step to maintain capture-avoidance.
- Beta-reduction is the fundamental computational mechanism in [[Lambda calculus | lambda calculus]], enabling the evaluation of function applications by substituting arguments into function bodies, and it is based on the substitution mechanism, which must respect variable bindings to avoid capturing free variables in the argument term.
- The beta-reduction process involves the replacement of a beta-redex, or reducible expression, with the term obtained by substituting the argument term for all free occurrences of the variable in the function body, and this substitution must respect variable bindings to avoid capturing free variables in the argument term.

## Church-Rosser Theorem and Confluence Implications
- The one-step beta-reduction relation, denoted as →β, applies this replacement to exactly one redex in a term, leaving the rest unchanged, and it is used to evaluate function applications by substituting arguments into function bodies, as illustrated in the examples of (λx.xy)z and (λx.(λy.yx))w.
- The lambda calculus utilizes multi-step beta-reduction, denoted as →β∗, which chains zero or more one-step reductions to allow full evaluation until no redexes remain, ultimately reaching a normal form if it exists, and the order of selecting redexes affects efficiency but not the final normal form in confluent systems.
- There are different reduction strategies, including normal-order reduction, which prioritizes the leftmost outermost redex, and applicative-order reduction, which evaluates arguments first by reducing innermost redexes, and these strategies illustrate beta-reduction's flexibility in modeling computation without altering the underlying substitution rule.

## Church Numerals and Arithmetic Operations
- Eta-conversion, also known as the η-rule, is a fundamental equivalence relation in [[Lambda calculus | lambda calculus]] that captures functional extensionality by identifying lambda terms that denote the same function despite differing in form, and it was introduced by [[Alonzo Church]] as the third postulate of lambda-conversion in his foundational work.
- The purpose of eta-conversion is to equate functions based on their input-output behavior, ensuring that two terms are considered identical if they produce the same result for every possible argument, thereby enforcing extensional equality in the untyped lambda calculus, and it allows the elimination of redundant abstractions that merely forward arguments.
- Eta-reduction specifically refers to the directional application of the eta-conversion rule for term simplification, contracting λx.(Mx) to M to achieve more concise representations, often used in normalization processes, and when combined with beta-reduction, eta-conversion extends the equivalence classes beyond mere computational steps to include extensionally equal functions.
- In lambda calculus, a term is in normal form if it contains no β-redexes, meaning no further β-reductions can be performed on it, and a weaker notion is the weak head normal form (WHNF), which requires only that the term has no β-redex at its head position, and reduction strategies specify the order in which redexes are selected for contraction to reach a normal form systematically.
- Call-by-name, also called normal-order reduction, prioritizes the leftmost outermost redex, substituting the argument unevaluated into the function body before reducing further, while call-by-value, or applicative-order reduction, first reduces all arguments of an application to normal form before performing the β-reduction, and these strategies have different advantages and disadvantages in terms of avoiding unnecessary computations and duplicating work.

## Boolean and Pair Encodings
- The Church-Rosser theorem is a key guarantee in [[Lambda calculus | lambda calculus]] that ensures if a term has a normal form, then any two reduction sequences from it will converge to α-equivalent normal forms, regardless of the strategy used, such as call-by-name or call-by-value, which is crucial for the confluence property.
- Confluence in lambda calculus is the property that ensures the independence of reduction order, meaning that if a term reduces via beta-reduction in zero or more steps to terms A and B, then there exists a term C such that A and B can both reduce to C, which guarantees that different reduction paths from the same starting term can reconverge.
- The Church-Rosser theorem was originally proved by [[Alonzo Church]] and [[J. Barkley Rosser]] in 1936, and later simplified by Tait and Martin-Löf, and it states that beta-reduction, and with eta-conversion, the combined beta-eta reduction, is confluent, which implies that should a term possess a normal form, this normal form is unique across all possible reduction sequences.

## Recursion and Fixed-Point Combinators
- The confluence property has significant implications, including the semantic equivalence of terms convertible via beta and eta reductions, and it extends to typed variants, such as the simply typed lambda calculus, where confluence follows from strong normalization, which means that every well-typed term reduces to a unique normal form in finitely many steps.
- Church numerals are a way of encoding natural numbers as higher-order functions in the untyped [[Lambda calculus | lambda calculus]], which enables the representation and manipulation of arithmetic without relying on primitive numerical types, and they are defined as lambda terms that take a function and a base value, then apply the function exactly n times to the base value, where n is the natural number being represented.
- The Church numeral n is defined as the lambda term λf.λx.fnx, where fnx denotes the n-fold iterated application of the function f to the argument x, and this approach highlights the [[Alan Turing | Turing]] completeness of lambda calculus by showing that even simple arithmetic is computable within the system, which was introduced by Alonzo Church as part of his development of lambda calculus as a foundation for logic and mathematics.
- The lambda calculus utilizes Church numerals, which are encoded as higher-order functions, to represent natural numbers, with the numeral for one being defined as λf.λx.fx, and higher numerals following a similar pattern, such as λf.λx.f(fx) for two.
- The successor function, S, is encoded as λn.λf.λx.f(nfx), which produces the next numeral by inserting an additional application of f around the n-fold application, yielding n+1, and this definition allows the construction of all natural numbers starting from zero by repeated application.

## Recursion Examples and Non-Termination
- Arithmetic operations, such as addition and multiplication, are defined combinatorially using beta-reduction, with addition encoded by the term λm.λn.λf.λx.mf(nfx), and multiplication given by λm.λn.λf.m(nf), where nfnf represents the n-fold composition of f.
- The predecessor function, which computes n-1 for n>0, and subtraction via repeated predecessors, rely on an auxiliary encoding of ordered pairs to track intermediate states during reduction, and this method avoids infinite loops by leveraging the finite structure of lambda terms to simulate decrementing.
- Booleans are encoded as higher-order functions that select one of two provided arguments, with true defined as λx.λy.x and false defined as λx.λy.y, and these combinators form the basis for a conditional construct, if, encoded as λp.λa.λb.pab, which applies the boolean p to the then-branch a and else-branch b.

## Church-Turing Thesis and Undecidability
- However, in the untyped lambda calculus, recursion via fixed points offers no guarantees of termination, as terms like the omega term may diverge, and even well-formed programs can enter infinite reductions depending on the evaluation strategy, underscoring a fundamental limitation of the untyped lambda calculus.
- The simply typed lambda calculus extends the untyped lambda calculus by incorporating a type system that assigns types to terms, ensuring well-formed expressions and preventing inconsistencies, but at the cost of expressiveness, and providing a contrast to the untyped variant.
- The simply typed lambda calculus, introduced by [[Alonzo Church]] in 1940, is a system that uses a simple type discipline with base types and function types to guarantee that every well-typed term terminates under beta-reduction, providing a foundation for safer computation models.
- Types in the simply typed [[Lambda calculus | lambda calculus]] are formed from a set of base types, such as ι for individuals or oo for truth values, and arrow types τ→σ, where τ and σ are types, representing functions from arguments of type τ to results of type σ, and typing judgments take the form Γ⊢M:τ, where Γ is a context, M is a lambda term, and τ is a type.

## Decidability and Complexity in Lambda Calculus

- The simply typed lambda calculus enjoys strong normalization, which means that every well-typed term has a normal form, and no infinite reduction sequence exists from it, and this property implies that evaluation always terminates for typed terms, contrasting with the undecidability of normalization in the untyped case.

## Functional Programming Features and Language Design
- Variants inspired by linear logic, such as Jean-Yves Girard's linear logic developed in 1987, introduce resource-sensitive typing disciplines, restricting the duplication and deletion of assumptions to model controlled resource usage, and leading to corresponding lambda calculi where variables must be used exactly once, with linear function types A ⊸ B enforcing that the argument A is consumed without copying.

- [[Lambda calculus]] is Turing complete, meaning it can simulate any Turing machine, thereby expressing the full power of general computation, and this equivalence arises from encodings where a Turing machine's tape is represented as a list, its states as higher-order functions, and transitions as lambda applications.
- The undecidability of the halting problem extends to lambda calculus, where no lambda term can determine whether another term reduces to a normal form for all inputs, and this result underscores lambda calculus's inability to resolve termination universally, mirroring limitations in other computability models.

## Pattern Matching and Modern Language Features
- The fixed-point theorem in lambda calculus, which guarantees the existence of fixed points for any term via combinators like the Y combinator, has profound implications for decidability of termination properties, and enables encoding of general recursive functions, including non-terminating ones, thereby reducing the halting problem to lambda term evaluation and rendering non-termination detection undecidable in the untyped setting.
- The concept of anonymous functions in lambda calculus, which are defined inline for immediate use without explicit names, has directly influenced the development of anonymous functions in programming languages, such as [[Lisp (programming language) | Lisp]] and JavaScript, where developers can create short, unnamed functions on the fly without declaring named procedures, as introduced by [[John McCarthy (computer scientist) | John McCarthy]].
- Higher-order functions, a cornerstone of functional programming, stem from the first-class status of functions in lambda calculus, where lambda terms can serve as arguments to other terms or be returned as results, enabling the construction of functions that manipulate other functions, such as applying a given function across a collection of values, a concept that has been incorporated into modern programming languages.

## Lambda Calculus in Contemporary Languages
- The [[Lambda calculus | lambda calculus]] has significantly influenced the design of functional programming languages by providing a formal foundation for function abstraction, application, and higher-order functions, with languages like [[Haskell]] directly incorporating its principles to emphasize referential transparency and equational reasoning.
- In functional programming implementations inspired by lambda calculus, closures package a function with its lexical environment, allowing inner functions to reference outer variables even after the outer scope exits, which supports modular code design by preserving context without global variables.

## Foundational Texts and Theoretical Frameworks
- Currying in lambda calculus transforms multi-argument functions into nested single-argument abstractions, permitting partial application by supplying arguments incrementally, and this technique is rooted in the unary nature of lambda abstractions, enabling the creation of specialized functions from general ones.
- Lambda calculus has underpinned the development of pure functional languages like Haskell, which adopts lazy evaluation corresponding to normal-order reduction, and impure variants like Scala and F#, which integrate lambda calculus concepts with object-oriented features to enable practical software development.
- The influence of [[Lambda calculus | lambda calculus]] can also be seen in mainstream languages like [[Python]] and Rust, which have adopted lambda-inspired constructs for concise expression of anonymous functions and closures, and languages like [[Haskell]] have built on this foundation to facilitate mathematical reasoning via β- and η-reduction.

## Lambda Calculus in Complexity Theory
- The design of languages like Haskell, Scala, and F# has been shaped by lambda calculus, with Haskell's non-strict semantics enabling compositional programming and equational optimizations, and Scala and F# blending lambda calculus principles with imperative and object-oriented paradigms for broader applicability.
- Researchers like Church and McCarthy have contributed to the development of lambda calculus, with McCarthy's apply mechanism in [[Lisp (programming language) | Lisp]] exemplifying the treatment of functions as data that can be passed and invoked dynamically, and Church's lambda calculus providing a foundation for denotational semantics and lazy evaluation.

## Conclusion and Broader Implications
- The [[Lambda calculus | lambda calculus]] has also extended to features like pattern matching in functional languages, and its principles have been applied in domain-specific embedded languages like Fran for functional reactive programming, demonstrating its profound impact on the design of functional programming languages.
- The lambda calculus has influenced the development of various programming languages, which use lambda-inspired constructs to enhance expressiveness, such as Scala's anonymous functions and F#'s fun keyword, allowing for the seamless mixing of functional purity with mutable state.
- Denotational semantics, pioneered by the Scott-Strachey approach in the 1960s, employs lambda calculus to define the meaning of programs as mathematical functions over domains, including complete partial orders that model computable values and fixed points, with semantic functions mapping syntactic constructs to denotations in domains like environments and stores.
